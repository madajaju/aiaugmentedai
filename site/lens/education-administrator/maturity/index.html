6 Levels of Maturity

The Maturity Ladder

Follow the sequence to build reliable, evidence-backed AI capability.

Level 1

Aware

You see the institutional challenge, but the response is still emerging.

The goal is to connect AI to strategy, policy, and student outcomes.

Example Scenario

"The Dean of a Liberal Arts college reads about AI's impact on higher ed. She convenes a town hall to discuss the institutional response."

Recommended Tools

  • Policy reviews, leadership briefings, and assessment results are useful starting tools.
View Level Details

Level 2

Exploring

The institution is comparing options and use cases.

The goal is to reduce confusion and establish direction.

Example Scenario

"A school district superintendent pilots AI in two middle schools. They use the 'AAOS Readiness Criteria' to compare how different schools handle student AI use."

Recommended Tools

  • Guidance documents and evaluation criteria help you test patterns.
View Level Details

Level 3

Experimenting

There are pilots, but not yet a stable operating model.

The goal is to turn experiments into a repeatable institutional path.

Example Scenario

"A university provost establishes an 'AI Ethics Board'. They require all AI pilots in administrative offices to use a 'Validation Ladder' for data privacy."

Recommended Tools

  • Pilot templates and implementation notes support repeatable practice.
View Level Details

Level 4

Integrating

AI is part of the institutions operating reality.

The goal is to make AI part of a dependable workflow.

Example Scenario

"A vocational school integrates AI into its career counseling services. Every student's 'Augmented Career Path' is backed by a verified 'Integrity Packet' of their skills."

Recommended Tools

  • Shared platforms and adoption support help maintain quality.
View Level Details

Level 5

Leading

The institution is aligning around a shared standard.

The goal is to make the path durable across programs and roles.

Example Scenario

"The head of a global school network standardizes AI policy across 50 campuses. They use 'Trust Velocity' to measure how quickly faculty adopt safe AI practices."

Recommended Tools

  • Operating model documents and governance processes are central.
View Level Details

Level 6

Augmenting

The institution is materially different because of AI.

The goal is to sustain augmentation without losing human-centered judgment.

Example Scenario

"An innovative 'Micro-school' network is built on an augmented operating model. Their entire administration, from admissions to graduation, is designed for human-AI partnership."

Recommended Tools

  • A stable governance stack and shared implementation language support this stage.
View Level Details

Core Model

The 6 Maturity Levels

The AAOS maturity ladder describes how a person, team, or organization becomes more capable of using AI well over time.

Level General Meaning Diagnostic Signal
Aware Need for AI is visible, but work is mostly manual. Can describe the gap, but not yet close it consistently.
Exploring AI tried in limited cases; learning what helps/fails. Curiosity and first attempts, but no repeatable control.
Experimenting Useful patterns begin to repeat; more deliberate use. Early standardization, but reliability varies by context.
Integrating AI is part of workflow; grounding/validation is routine. Work moves through normal operations without reinvention.
Leading Practice is strong enough to teach or standardize. System can absorb variation while preserving quality.
Augmenting Human expertise and AI are a durable partnership. Reliable enough to improve, transfer, and scale safely.
